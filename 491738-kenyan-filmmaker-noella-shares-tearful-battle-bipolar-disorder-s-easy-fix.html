Battle with mental health begins

Noella Luka, an award-winning filmmaker, emotionally shared her personal experience with bipolar disorder.

Scholarship to the US

She began to shape her career path when she would use her family members as subjects to tell her stories.

"My father, mother and two elder brothers were my first subjects in storytelling," she said, as recorded by BBC News Africa.

She landed a lucrative scholarship to study filmmaking in the US, where she experienced her first manic episode.

"One day I woke up excited. I wanted to make a feast for my family. I called them saying how excited I was that they were coming to see me. But no one was coming. There was no party. Everything went blank," she recalled.

Journey back to Kenya

She was forced to quit school and return home to Kenya to be with her family members.

Her mother who was battling colon cancer was optimistic her daughter would be cured and "her dream will be valid".

"I have been diagnosed with bipolar disorder. I don't fully know what it means, but one thing I keep experiencing is not being able to distinguish reality and what isn't," Noella narrated.

Seeking spiritual reconciliation

Her family turned to the church for guidance, as they believed God would work it out and cure their daughter.

"I am afraid to tell them. It may take more than that," Noella, a mental health advocate, said.

She added the medicine she took made her forget days as she could sleep the entire night and have enough sleep for the day.

"It's really frustrating because you cannot be productive. Welcome to my world," she said.

Boy with bipolar disorder goes missing

In a different story, a Nairobi mum hoped to reunite with her son with bipolar disorder who went missing in December.

Pauline Nyokabi said her son, Clarence Gitangu left home after he had been dropped off by Mathari National Teaching and Referral Hospital's social workers at 3:30 pm.
