With a cigar drooping from his mouth, a cocky expression on his face - and a gun in his hands, this is a fascinating glimpse of eccentric Penthouse founder Bob Guccione.

Deep in the realms of the treasure trove of his belongings, photos, artwork and documents, lies this evocative picture of Guccione posing with staff outside the Penthouse club he opened in London in 1965.

The New Yorker was born a Catholic and once even considered becoming a priest. But it was while living in London during the "Swinging Sixties" that he founded a magazine intended to rival Playboy - describing itself as "the magazine of sex, politics and protest".

In the early years, Guccione - an aspiring artist - photographed many of the models himself to save money - with his Minolta camera also up for auction - and Penthouse became the first publication to show full frontal nudity.

Tthe magazine made him one of the richest men in the world and in 2004, the New York Post estimated he had earned $4billion since Penthouse was launched in 1965.

However, by the time of his death from cancer aged 79 in Plano, Texas, in October 2010, Guccione was bankrupt, penniless from a string of bad investments and risky ventures. But Wall Street trader turned entrepreneur and financier Jeremy Frommer who bought Guccione's entire estate from his creditor last year. told MailOnline: 'I want to show the world both the brilliance and erotic beauty of the eye of Bob Guccione.'

Guccione's erotic empire began in London with a bank loan for $1,170 and a dream of outdoing Hugh Hefner's Playboy, competing by opening the Penthouse Club in Mayfair's Whitehorse Street - close to the Playboy Club's Park Lane base.

But almost immediately, he caused chaos by using an old mailing list - a 35-page document also to be auctioned off - so that promotional brochures with pornographic samples went out to clergymen, schoolgirls, old-age pensioners and wives of members of Parliament.

Despite the huge outcry, and a $264 fine for mailing indecent materials, all 120,000 copies of the first issue of Penthouse sold out in days, and Mr. Guccione, a struggling artist from New Jersey who had been living in Europe for more than a decade, was on his way to being a tycoon. He then opened Penthouse in the U.S in 1969 and it became one of the nation's most successful magazines.

As MailOnline has revealed, among the images found by Mr Frommer are never-before published images of Madonna as an 18-year-old, posing in 1977 for photographer Herman Kulkens.

Long-lost images of Arnold Schwarzenegger, President Bill Clinton's mistress Gennifer Flowers and Lauren Hutton have also been found.

Teenager: Madonna was just 18 in 1977 when she posed nude for photographer Herman Kulkens. The pictures would be published less than 10 years later when she became a star

Mr Frommer is now putting the Madonna images up for auction on November 9 on Guccionecollection.com and told MailOnline: 'We will also be selling 21 35mm slides, only six have ever been published...the craziest thing is that we still don't know who the mystery girl whose hair Madonna's playing with is.

Jeremy also revealed how he has been turned away from top auction houses he labels as 'stodgy' who have refused to sell many of the erotic images from Guiccione's estate.

He said: 'Auction houses have been more than willing to auction off Hugh Hefner's memorabilia and to auction off Jeff Koons artwork, yet every single auction house that we approached was afraid and fearful and while the reception we originally got from the people directly involved in the business was general excitement over the material, the stodgy older auction house elite refused to give Bob Guccione the credit he deserves. So I have decided to do it myself.'

Among the images going up for auction are the original pictures of a blonde woman printed in an article in Penthouse in 1980 which claimed the photos - uncovered by a Swedish photographer - were of a young Marilyn Monroe in an untitled, silent six-minutes porn film.

However, no Monroe biographer has ever found any evidence that the iconic actress ever filmed such a movie - with the women pictured bearing little resemblance to the Hollywood actress.

Despite this, Penthouse in its article insisted it was her, writing; 'Here, in grainy celluloid, may well be the still unglamorized sex goddess the public never knew, before plastic surgeons, stylists, and designers transformed her into the mythical Marilyn Monroe.
