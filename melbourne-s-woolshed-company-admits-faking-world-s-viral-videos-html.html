Melbourne's Woolshed Company admits faking some of world's most viral videos

 Posted on July 7, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  891 words  |  Martina Birk

They duped hundreds of millions of people with their viral hoax videos – but the production company responsible has defended their actions as a 'social experiment.'

Melbourne-based Woolshed Company has admitted being behind some of the most famous viral videos of recent years, from a woman narrowly avoiding a bolt of lightning in Sydney's Tamarama to a man walking away into an outback tornado.

The eight stitch-up videos, funded in part by Screen Australia, amassed more than of 205 million views and coverage from news outlets in over 180 countries the world over.

Managing director and co-founder of The Woolshed Company Dave Christison told Daily Mail Australia he denied crossing any ethical boundaries.

'None of the videos were malicious and I think most people who watched them did so with a grain of salt. This was an experiment in creating short, snackable entertainment that could reach a worldwide audience.'

The first clip showed what appeared to be a man's close encounter with a great white shark at Sydney's Manly – amassing an impressive 40 million views.

But despite getting some return from YouTube advertising, Mr Christison said the cost of producing the clips far exceeded the return.

'The videos cost anywhere between $AUD20,000 - $70,000 to make. To put that in perspective, if you had a video that hit five million views in its critical period, the first week, you'd be lucky to pull in a couple of grand.'

They used a range of production techniques, from imposing stock footage over their recordings - as in the case of the shark, lion and bear videos – to complex CGI animations, as used in the outback tornado.

To help bankroll the project they had help from Screen Australia Multi Platform Fund, who offered more than $100,000 to production costs.

The most successful clip showed a couple's close call with lightning when a bolt appeared to strike just metres from a woman in Sydney's Tamarama – garnering an impressive 59 million clicks.

The team was constantly developing concept creation approaches, production techniques, and distribution methods to discover the formula for shareable content.

'Danger works, animals, close encounters with dangerous animals work. We tried play with humour in some of them, the risk of danger seems to work stronger than others,' said Mr Christison.

'Kids and animals doing funny stuff is a safe bet, but this can often be difficult to fake.'

FROM CGI SHARKS TO LIGHTNING BOLT GRAPHICS: HOW THEY MADE THE FAKE VIRAL VIDEOS 

  • In the video showing the man diving into the water before coming face to face with a great white shark, they used original footage of an actor diving into the water with a GoPro then superimposed stock footage of a shark over this with CGI 
  • In the video showing a man taking a selfie in front of a tornado in the Outback, they used original footage of an actor then superimposed CGI of a tornado 
  • In the video showing the couple narrowly avoiding being struck by lightning, they used original footage of actors walking by the water then superimposed a graphic stock of a lightning bolt 
  • In the video showing a snowboarder being chased by a bear, they used original footage of an actor in the snow then superimposed stock footage of a bear with CGI 
  • In the video showing a lion attacking a trophy hunter, they used original footage of actors then superimposed stock footage of the lions with CGI 
  • In selfie stick fight clip video, they used original footage of actors fighting on a ship without any special effects  
  • In the video showing the GoPro falling off a drone into a dance floor at Burning Man Festival, they used editing techniques to make the camera look as if it was tumbling from the sky
  • In the video showing the Stormtrooper toppling down the stairs, they used a stuntman to fall over and did not use special effects
